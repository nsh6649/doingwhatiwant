Megan Thee Stallion Keeps Her Business To Herself Nandi Howard It's been over two years since Megan Thee Stallion and I have spoken. As a journalist from Houston, Texas, I was granted the opportunity to interview the entertainer right after I graduated college in 2017. At the time, Megan was going viral on Twitter for participating in freestyle cyphers made up of local rappers in the city. As I transitioned to New York for the start of my career, Megan soon signed her record deal, and we reconnected on the set of her first single, "Big Ole Freak." I was invited by her late mother Holly Thomas to the New York taping, however, they ended up shooting the video two additional times. What I saw then is much of what the world is actualizing now - Megan Thee Stallion is an undeniable powerhouse. "Megan says she's familiar with you," her PR says as I walk into her cover shoot for this magazine. To my surprise (and an unlikely happening in the city), a major magazine was shooting their cover here in Houston. Prior to the Monday morning shoot, Megan had an eventful weekend in our hometown. Just the day before, she received the 18th Congressional District Humanitarian Award from Congresswoman Sheila Jackson Lee. The prestigious award is given to people in the city who help, without the need for anyone to ask. "The district has almost 1 million people. When this award is given, it has that special promise and special history," Lee exclaimed at the intimate press conference. And the day before that, she walked across the stage as she graduated from HBCU Texas Southern University with a B.A. in Health Administration. The rapper hopes to use her degree to open assisted living facilities in Houston and hire recent college graduates, aiming to provide job opportunities for those who are struggling after college. Our interview happened after her second look wrapped. She was wearing a custom Coach bikini and mini skirt, and at that moment, I couldn't help but get emotional. A Southern, all-natural, thick thighed and slim waisted darling is at the peak of representation dialogue in both fashion and music. When we speak, Megan is relaxing in the greenroom in a white fleece robe. Her glam was the main attraction; a silk-melted, long, black lace wig and camera-ready makeup. "Hey girl," she says while giving me a hug and typing to a fan on Twitter. "I always take the little time I can to respond to my supporters," she explains as I quickly set up my laptop. While I don't share the same rigorous schedule as, we agree that responding to everyone on social media can be time consuming. Although there is a familiarity between us, my nerves began to rise, because what was once a viral sensation in 2017 is now a global powerhouse. I wasn't just talking to Megan Pete from Houston's Southside who likes to get a Brisk tea from Texaco (a popular gas station in Texas), I was speaking with Megan Thee Stallion; a groundbreaking entertainer who has partnerships with Popeyes, Crunchyroll, Revlon, Netflix, songs with the likes of Beyoncé, Nicki Minaj, and Ariana Grande, a crew too numerous to count on her payroll, and a fan base that is growing by the day. Megan is originally from Houston's Yellowstone where she lived with her grandmother. She attended Kandy Stripe Academy on MLK Blvd in the Southpark area, an adjacent neighborhood to hers, and went to Allen Thomas Middle School. When her father came home from jail and started "making money," as she recalls, Megan eventually went to high school in Pearland - a suburb outside of Houston. We both related to this sentiment; Houston's better schools are traditionally in the suburbs, and as it's not uncommon to use a family member's address to acquire a higher quality of education. Her upbringing is efficiently tied to her raps. "You are listening to me obviously, because you want to step into my world. So I just want to put my Houston flavor in everybody's ear," she says. On her latest project Something For The Hotties, released at the end of last year, she reminds listeners she's still the same girl on "Southside Forever Freestyle," rapping, "out in Calabasas doing ratchet shit." Much of Megan's music has distinct samples from popular rappers down south like Pimp C and Bun B, and she uses Houston colloquialisms to top off her rhymes. "I hear certain things, certain samples, I'm like, 'Yo, that's so Houston, that's so Southside.'" But among all the accomplishments of the 3-time Grammy Award winning rapper, Megan seemed the most visibly excited about her completed education plan. After all, she details this is what her late mother and grandmother wanted: "I feel very, very proud. I'm still excited," she says with a smile on her face. "I accomplish things, and I don't really get to live in the moment, because I'm like, 'Okay, I got to do this next. I got to catch the flight, and we got to go over here now.' But this is something that I've been working at for a long, long time." The rapper started her college journey at Prairie View A&M University, a nearby college about 45 minutes from Houston, but withdrew after her career began to blow up. Megan, however, continued to push to finish school, going to Houston Community College and the University of Houston downtown before ending up at TSU. "When I got to TSU, I actually started going on campus, and then everything really took off," she says. It's hard to believe Megan Thee Stallion is still at the beginning of her career. There's no denying she's a contemporary pop star, although she will quickly remind you that "I'm a rapper." But to say she is in her prime is to undermine the projection the Houston talent is brewing. "I'm just a naturally confident person, when I put my mind to something, I got to do it. I said I was going to do it, so I got to follow through. I be praying about it, I be putting the work in, and it happens," she says. Megan Thee Stallion's music remains fun. And she herself is always full of energy. Her "What's up hotties, it's Megan Thee Stallion, A.K.A the hot girl coach" intro she does on her promo videos, articulates her in-person charisma – loveable, pleasant, and a southern-belle agreeableness to captivate you even more. Despite what may look like an overnight success, the proof is all over the internet that Megan built her career from the ground up on her own terms. She came up during a time when organic ways of finding artists online was seemingly a thing of the past. Labels were getting smarter, and artists like Playboi Carti, Trippie Redd, and 21 Savage were able to grab major deals from homemade music, making their most viral songs in makeshift studios. Megan was able to dodge initial major label offers in the beginning and worked as an independent artist, putting out mixtapes like Make It Hot and Tina Snow and lighting the viral world on fire with her Instagram and Twitter freestyles. "My whole career, nobody handed it to me. It was something that I chose to do and I really wanted it, and I really love it," she says. "I love to rap. I love to dance. I just love to work, I love the grind. And I feel like my mama was definitely a hard working woman. She liked to grind. So I feel like I get a lot of my work ethic from her." With a rapidly rising career, the time finally came to sign with 300 Entertainment, where she released her critically acclaimed tape Fever in 2019, featuring her first Billboard Hot 100 placement, "Big Ole Freak," which debuted at 99 before peaking to 69. Fever also introduced the world to her relationship with iconic rapper Juicy J and Houston producer, and longtime collaborator Lil Ju Made The Beat. She quickly followed the release with an EP titled Suga, which featured "Savage," a track that would grow to epic proportions with a remix adding an iconic Beyoncé feature. But just a year after Megan signed her record deal, while she was holding back-to-back live performances and was celebrating the success of her hit song "Hot Girl Summer" with Nicki Minaj and Ty Dolla $ign, the world went into lockdown, forcing all artists to shift their gameplan. For Megan, this meant heading back to her familiar space - the internet. She once again began to post freestyles from her new spot on the West Coast, utilized TikTok, and put out her debut album proper, Good News. As a fan, I originally felt that Good News deserved a bigger push from 300, but Megan kindly reminded me that she humbly recorded her debut album from home during the pandemic. "I feel like Good News is awesome," she says. "Of course everything crazy in the world was happening. I made it in the house, in the living room. I don't have any regrets about it." Just as Megan was enjoying her quarantine glow-up, which included her debut album release and #1 Billboard charting song, she found herself in a heavily publicized incident resulting in the rapper being shot in the foot by at the time she thought was a trusted friend. In our previous interviews early throughout her career, Megan was comfortable with only maintaining friendships with her longtime friends. "No matter where you work at, what you do, you just got to learn. Everything that grin ain't your friend," she exclaimed. While the court case is still on-going, Megan details how her relationship with industry insiders has evolved. "I really had to learn the same thing your mama has been telling you all your life, 'everybody ain't your friend, everything they grin ain't your friend,'" Megan exclaims. "I feel like definitely coming up, I was so stiff, I tried to be open and I try to be nice, because I'm a nice person. I'm an Aquarius. I'm very much a people person. I'm all over the place. I had to learn, reel it in a little bit, because everybody's motives aren't the same." It's an industry standard for celebrities who go through public scandals to eventually talk about it. Whether it's an intense one-on-one interview with Gayle King or a Youtube intervention with Charlamagne Tha God, we've become accustomed to seeing celebrities detailing their trauma. Yet Megan Thee Stallion has primarily, defiantly, stayed quiet. From the death of her mother and grandmother to being a victim of gun violence, she has experienced a great deal of loss at the start of her career, and she's only just beginning to feel comfortable enough to open up. "As a Black woman, you hear your mama tell you all the time, 'Don't put nobody in my business.' So it's always been on my mind that everybody doesn't need to know my business." As Megan Thee Stallion becomes larger than life, Megan Pete is left with actualizing the turmoil that has come with her unscalable success. There's no blueprint on how to be a college student one minute and a global superstar the next, but throughout scandal and success, Megan is steadily making her own rules, on her own time. As Black women, we are often pressured to detail our pain, in part to make it more believable. And while Megan Thee Stallion has accomplished great success, the rapper has also experienced synchronal heartbreak. "I'm just now starting to get more comfortable with letting people know a little something, because these days, if I don't talk about anything personal, I see a lot of people trying to talk for me and tell my story. And they're assuming things, you can say anything you want to say on the internet. And if it's a lot, it sounds more interesting than the truth." Megan leads with authority by telling her story, and perhaps that's why she has been able to bulldoze her way into the industry so effortlessly. Megan Thee Stallion is entirely self-made. And as she stops the interview to remind me, you better not forget what she is first and foremost: "I definitely am not a pop girl. I always say I'm a real rap bitch, ain't no pop shit."
